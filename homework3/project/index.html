  • Video games

    A video game is an electronic game that involves interaction with a user interface to generate visual feedback on a two- or three-dimensional video display device such as a TV screen, virtual reality headset or computer monitor. Since the 1980s, video games have become an increasingly important part of the entertainment industry, and whether they are also a form of art is a matter of dispute. The electronic systems used to play video games are called platforms. Video games are developed and released for one or several platforms and may not be available on others. Specialized platforms such as arcade games, which present the game in a large, typically coin-operated chassis, were common in the 1980s in video arcades, but declined in popularity as other, more affordable platforms became available. These include dedicated devices such as video game consoles, as well as general-purpose computers like a laptop, desktop or handheld computing devices. The input device used for games, the game controller, varies across platforms. Common controllers include gamepads, joysticks, mouse devices, keyboards, the touchscreens of mobile devices, or even a person's body, using a Kinect sensor. Players view the game on a display device such as a television or computer monitor or sometimes on virtual reality head-mounted display goggles. There are often game sound effects, music and voice actor lines which come from loudspeakers or headphones. Some games in the 2000s include haptic, vibration-creating effects, force feedback peripherals and virtual reality headsets. In the 2010s, the commercial importance of the video game industry is increasing. The emerging Asian markets and mobile games on smartphones in particular are driving the growth of the industry. As of 2018, video games generated sales of US$134.9 billion annually worldwide, and were the third-largest segment in the U.S. entertainment market, behind broadcast and cable TV.

  • Radio equipment development and formation

    Typical process for designing a printed circuit board according to a ready-made circuit diagram:
    Preparation for design: Import a circuit diagram into a CAD database for a circuit board. As a rule, the preparation of the circuit is performed in a separate CAD circuit. Some CAD packages contain components of both circuitry and design. Other PCB CAD systems do not have a circuit editor in their composition, only importing a circuit diagram of popular formats. Input of components into CAD (drawings of each component, location and purpose of outputs, etc.). Usually, they use off-the-shelf component libraries provided by CAD developers. Clarification by the future manufacturer of the printed circuit board of its technological capabilities (available materials, number of layers, accuracy class, permissible hole diameters, the possibility of coatings, etc.). Based on these data, a preliminary selection is made of the board material, the number of metallization layers, the thickness of the material and foil, the accuracy class and they are also the initial data for configuring the DRC (see below) used both for automatic wiring and for checking the diluted board. Most often, optimal foil fiberglass 1.5 mm thick with a foil thickness of 18 or 35 microns.
    Board Design: Determination of the design of the printed circuit board (contour and overall dimensions, mounting holes, maximum permissible height of the components). Drawing dimensions (edges) of the board, cutouts and mounting holes, areas of the prohibition of the placement of components. Placement of structurally attached parts: connectors, indicators, buttons, etc. Defining the rules for the location of critical conductors: identifying areas for laying high-current conductors and power buses; arrangement of high-frequency and differential lines, determination of methods for laying and shielding noise-sensitive circuits and circuits - sources of interference. Perform automatic or manual component placement. Usually they tend to place all components on one side of the board, since two-sided installation of parts is noticeably more expensive to manufacture. Start automatic tracer. In case of unsatisfactory result - re-placement of components. These two steps are often performed tens or hundreds of times in a row. In some cases, tracing of printed circuit boards (drawing tracks) is done manually in whole or in part. Checking the board for errors (DRC, Design Rules Check): checking for gaps, short circuits, component overlays, etc. In some cases, the calculation of the mechanical properties of the resulting printed circuit board is required: the frequency of intrinsic mechanical resonance and impact strength. If necessary, change the reference points of the board or the placement of heavy components.
    Creation of output design documentation: Export the file to a format accepted by the PCB manufacturer, for example, Gerber; Design documentation, in accordance with current standards, which specifies the technical requirements for the manufacture of printed circuit boards: the name of the material of the foil base, the drilling diameters of all types of holes, the type of vias (closed with a solder mask or open, tinned), areas of galvanic coatings and their type, color solder mask and its type, the need for marking, the method of processing the circuit board (milling or scribing), etc. In agreement with the manufacturer, the requirements may indicate camping in the accompanying note.
